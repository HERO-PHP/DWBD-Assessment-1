## 1.Introduction

**Business Background**：Grab is Southeast Asia's leading super app. Its GrabMart business provides grocery and daily necessities delivery to Singaporean users through partnerships with retailers and Dark Stores. Currently, Grab faces challenges in integrating the massive amounts of transaction data generated by its various independent systems (logistics, merchant management, payments, etc.), making it difficult for management to conduct unified decision analysis.

**Core issues and objectives**：Due to fluctuating demand, GrabMart faces the challenge of both stockouts of popular items and stockpiles of slow-moving goods. Business analysts recommended designing and implementing a data warehouse solution, focusing on inventory management processes, to improve inventory accuracy and transparency, thereby optimizing operating costs and enhancing customer satisfaction.

**Core issues and objectives**：
Inventory management involves three key stages:

* **Purchase Ordering:** When inventory falls below a threshold, place an order with the supplier, recording the order quantity, date, product, and supplier information.
* **Receiving and Warehousing:** Receive goods from the supplier and update the received quantity and inventory records.
* **Inventory Control:** Maintain accurate inventory levels at each location, including new product arrivals, existing inventory updates, and location tracking.

**Data analysis needs**：
Management needs to leverage the data warehouse to achieve the following analytical capabilities:

* **Inventory Monitoring:** View daily product inventory levels and weekly reports on reaching minimum inventory thresholds.

* **Multi-dimensional Analysis:** Analyze inventory status by brand, category, supplier, and other dimensions.

* **Purchase Fulfillment Analysis:** Generate reports on the sending and receiving of purchase orders over the past four weeks, and evaluate receipt status monthly or by supplier.

**Additional Information**：
The solution will integrate data from GrabMart's inventory and supplier management system, including supplier details, product information, inventory levels, and inventory transfer records between suppliers and warehouses/stores.

In the fields of big data processing and traditional business intelligence (BI), Apache HDFS and SQL Server (SSMS)-based data warehouses represent two distinct architectures. The following is a summary of their advantages and disadvantages:

First, Apache HDFS: The Cornerstone of Distributed Storage

Advantages:

* **Extremely High Scalability:**
  HDFS uses a distributed architecture, enabling easy scaling to petabytes (PB) or even exabytes (EB) of data by adding inexpensive general-purpose servers <sup>[1][2]</sup>.

* **High Fault Tolerance:**
  Through a multi-replica mechanism, HDFS can automatically handle node failures, ensuring data availability even in the event of hardware damage <sup>[1][3]</sup>.

* **Support for Unstructured Data:**
  It does not require a predefined schema (Schema-on-Read) and can store data in various raw formats such as videos, logs, and images, making it an ideal choice for building "data lakes" <sup>[2][4]</sup>.

Disadvantages:

* **High Latency:**
  It is designed for high throughput, not low-latency access, and is not suitable for real-time point queries <sup>[1][3]</sup>.

* **Operational Complexity:**
  Lacking native robust indexing and ACID transaction support, it typically requires integration with components like Hive and Spark for data analysis <sup>[4][5]</sup>.

Secondly, SSMS (SQL Server) Data Warehouse: The Essence of Relational Architecture

Advantages:

* **High-Performance Queries:**
  Possesses a mature query optimizer, indexing mechanism, and columnstore indexes, performing exceptionally well in complex multi-table joins and report generation <sup>[6][7]</sup>.

* **Strict Security and Transactions:**
  Provides robust access control and ACID transaction guarantees, ensuring data accuracy and the rigor of business logic <sup>[6][8]</sup>.

* **Ease of Use and Ecosystem:**
  SSMS offers an intuitive graphical interface, and SQL is widely used, allowing seamless integration with analytics tools like Power BI <sup>[7][9]</sup>.

Disadvantages:

* **High Scaling Costs:**
  While SQL Server supports distributed architectures (such as PolyBase), its vertical scaling costs are significantly higher compared to HDFS horizontal scaling <sup>[6][10]</sup>.

* **Rigid Schema:**
  Strict schema-on-write design reduces flexibility when handling semi-structured or unstructured data <sup>[8][10]</sup>.

Apache HDFS is suitable as a data infrastructure for handling massive, diverse, and rapidly growing raw data; while SQL Server data warehouses are better suited for storing cleaned, highly structured core business data requiring frequent interactive analysis.

In modern enterprise architectures, the two are often complementary rather than mutually exclusive. A common best practice is to use HDFS to store massive amounts of raw data and then import high-value aggregated data into SQL Server via ETL processes, achieving a balance between cost, scalability, and analytical efficiency <sup>[4][9][10]</sup>.
